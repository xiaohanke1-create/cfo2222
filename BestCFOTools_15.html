What Do Financial Analysts Do?

What Do Financial Analysts Do? Learn about their roles, skills, and responsibilities in helping businesses make smart financial decisions.

Financial analysts evaluate financial data to help individuals and companies make informed decisions about investments, budgeting, and forecasting. They create models, analyze trends, assess risks, and provide strategic guidance for financial planning.

Still unsure if corporate finance or investments better match your strengths? That's a common challenge for aspiring finance professionals-especially with the pace of change in the modern finance industry.

According to the 2024 Datarails CFO Sentiment Survey, 57% of CFOs expect their finance department headcount to decrease by 2026 due to AI adoption, while only 16% anticipate growth, and 27% foresee no change.

So how can you confidently choose a financial analyst path that aligns with your strengths and sets you up for long-term success?

What Is a Financial Analyst and Why Are They Important?

A financial analyst evaluates financial data, monitors market trends, reviews company performance, and builds models to forecast future outcomes. Depending on their area of focus, they may analyze broad economic indicators or specialize in specific industries.

The role of a financial analyst is highly analytical and requires strong mathematical and critical thinking skills. These professionals work across various sectors, including investment banks, insurance companies, hedge funds, private equity firms, and government agencies.

Their insights support informed decision-making by identifying risks, uncovering opportunities, and shaping financial strategies. Businesses and investors depend on financial analysts to allocate resources effectively, boost performance, and plan for long-term growth. Their work directly influences the financial direction and overall success of an organization.

What Are the Financial Analyst Responsibilities?

Financial analysts support informed decision-making by analyzing data, guiding investments, and identifying opportunities for growth. Their goal is to help individuals and organizations allocate resources effectively. Common responsibilities include:

Provide Financial Advice

Review financial data to recommend effective strategies. This includes evaluating performance, identifying growth areas, and balancing risk and reward.

Research Market Trends

Analyze economic indicators, market activity, and industry developments to identify patterns, assess risks, and uncover potential opportunities. Some analysts specialize in specific sectors or regions for deeper insights.

Manage Portfolios and Funds

Oversee asset allocations and make buy-or-sell decisions on behalf of clients or organizations. Financial models and market data are used to maximize returns.

Evaluate Financial Performance and Risk

Assess the value and risk of investments or organizational performance using reports and metrics. Analysts help reveal insights that support better decision-making.

Develop Investment Strategies

Design investment plans based on financial goals, market conditions, and risk tolerance. These strategies help guide clients or organizations toward long-term financial success.

Required Skills and Qualifications for Financial Analysts

To succeed as a financial analyst, you need a strong combination of technical expertise, business insight, and analytical thinking. Generalists and business financial analysts alike are expected to interpret data, forecast outcomes, and support strategic decisions.  Here are the most important qualifications and skills required for this role:

Educational Background

Most financial analysts hold at least a bachelor's degree in one of the following fields:

  • Finance
  • Accounting
  • Economics
  • Business Administration
  • Statistics or Mathematics

For more advanced roles-especially in investment banking, corporate finance, or asset management-employers may prefer candidates with a master's degree in finance or an MBA.

Professional Certifications (Optional but Advantageous)

While not always required, certifications can strengthen your profile as a business financial analyst and open doors to higher-level opportunities:

  • CFA (Chartered Financial Analyst) - Highly respected in the investment industry.
  • CPA (Certified Public Accountant) - Ideal for those who work closely with financial reporting.
  • FRM (Financial Risk Manager) - Useful for roles involving risk management and assessment.

Technical Skills

Strong technical capabilities are a must for any financial analyst. Common tools and systems include:

  • Excel and spreadsheet modeling
  • Financial modeling and forecasting
  • Creation of visual representations to communicate data insights
  • ERP/accounting systems
  • Programming knowledge is optional but increasingly valuable for data-driven roles

Analytical and Critical Thinking

At the heart of a financial analyst's skills is the ability to extract insights from large datasets, spot patterns, and make sound recommendations. Analysts must process information objectively and deliver well-reasoned conclusions.

Communication and Presentation Skills

A successful financial analyst doesn't just crunch numbers-they also know how to explain their findings. Clear written and verbal communication is vital for reporting insights to clients, executives, or stakeholders in a persuasive and understandable way.

Attention to Detail and Time Management

Accuracy is everything. Even small errors can impact decisions or forecasts. That's why financial analyst skills also include being detail-oriented, highly organized, and able to manage multiple priorities under tight deadlines.

Types of Financial Analysts

Financial analysts can focus on various specialties based on their industry or area of expertise. Here are some of the most common roles within the field:

  • Investment Banking Analyst
  • Equity Research Analyst
  • Treasury Analyst
  • Financial Planning and Analysis (FP&A) Analyst
  • Private Equity Analyst
  • Corporate Development Analyst

Where Do They Work?

Financial analysts are employed across a wide range of industries and organizations. Their skills are in demand wherever data-driven financial decision-making is needed. The role of a financial analyst can vary depending on the company size, sector, and focus, but the core goal remains the same: to help businesses and individuals make smart financial choices.

You'll find professionals in analyst finance roles working in environments such as:

  • Investment banks - Assisting in mergers, acquisitions, and capital-raising strategies
  • Corporate finance departments - Supporting budgeting, forecasting, and long-term planning
  • Insurance companies - Evaluating risk and developing financial models
  • Mutual funds and hedge funds - Researching investment opportunities and managing portfolios
  • Private equity and venture capital firms - Assessing the financial health of potential investments
  • Government agencies and non-profits - Analyzing economic trends and managing public funds
  • Consulting firms - Advising clients on financial strategy and performance optimization

In all of these settings, financial analyst skills-such as data interpretation, financial modeling, and market analysis-play an important role in achieving financial goals. No matter the industry, the role of financial analyst remains an important part of strategic decision-making and resource planning.

Your Future in Finance Starts with One Decision

At its core, the role of a financial analyst is about guiding decisions that shape businesses, industries, and even entire economies. A career in finance offers many paths, each with its own challenges and rewards. If you're at a career crossroads, now is the time to roll up your sleeves and start analyzing the numbers that drive change.

Your future won't wait. Which financial path will you choose to master next?
